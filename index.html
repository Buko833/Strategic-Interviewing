Round 2: Behavioral Questions
Identify the best behavioral question

Context: You are interviewing candidates for a customer service position and want to understand how they handle conflict.

Your Task: Which question is the BEST behavioral question?

✓ Correct Answer: C) Tell me about a time...

This is a behavioral question that asks for a specific past example. It encourages STAR responses and provides evidence of actual behavior rather than hypothetical answers.

Round 3: Legal Questions
Identify illegal or problematic interview questions

Context: You are conducting interviews for a supervisory position.

Your Task: Which question is PROBLEMATIC or ILLEGAL?

✓ Correct Answer: B) Are you planning to have children?

This question is illegal under Title VII of the Civil Rights Act. It discriminates based on gender and family planning, which is not job-related. Avoid questions about marital status, children, age, religion, or national origin.

Round 4: Evidence vs Opinion
Distinguish between fact-based and opinion-based responses
Interviewer: "Tell me about your strongest skill for this role."
Candidate: "I think I am a naturally talented problem-solver. I am really good at figuring things out."
Your Task: What is the main issue with this response?

✓ Correct Answer: B) Lacks specific examples or evidence

This is an opinion-based response without concrete examples. A better response would include a specific situation where the candidate demonstrated problem-solving skills with measurable results.

Round 5: Probing Questions
Choose the best follow-up probe
Interviewer: "Tell me about a time you had to deliver bad news."
Candidate: "I had to tell a patient that a test came back positive for a serious condition."
Your Task: What is the best follow-up probe?

✓ Correct Answer: C) What specific approach did you take...

This probe seeks specific behavioral details and outcomes. It encourages the candidate to provide more information about their actual actions and results, which is evidence-based interviewing.

Round 6: Interview Notes
Evaluate what to document
During the interview, the candidate mentioned: "I really get along with everyone on my team. People like working with me and I have great energy."
Your Task: How should you document this response?

✓ Correct Answer: C) Described specific examples...

Document what you actually heard with specific examples, not interpretations or judgments. This maintains objectivity and provides evidence for hiring decisions. Avoid generalizations or subjective assessments.

Round 7: Question Construction
Build a strong interview question

Job Requirement: The role requires the ability to work independently and manage time effectively.

Your Task: Which question BEST assesses independent work and time management?

✓ Correct Answer: B) Tell me about a time you had to manage...

This is a strong behavioral question that directly relates to the job requirement. It asks for a specific past example where the candidate demonstrated the required skills with measurable outcomes.

Round 8: Multiple Mistakes
Identify all the problems in this interview
Interviewer: "You seem like a smart person, so I know you will be great at this job. By the way, are you married? Do you plan to stay in this city?"
Candidate: "Yes, I am married with two kids."
Interviewer: "Great! I am sure with your background, you will fit right in here."
Your Task: How many problems did the interviewer make?

✓ Correct Answer: C) 3 or more problems

Problems:

1. Making assumptions (smart person = great job fit)

2. Illegal questions about marital status and location plans

3. Judging based on background assumptions rather than evidence-based answers

4. Lack of objective criteria and behavioral questions
